Limits

Limits describe how a function behaves near a point, instead of at that point. This simple yet powerful idea is the basis of all of calculus.

What are Limits?

A limit is a value that a function approaches as the input (often denoted as \( x \)) gets closer to a certain value. We use limits to describe the behavior of a function at a specific point or as it approaches infinity.

Notation

The limit of a function \( f(x) \) as \( x \) approaches a value \( a \) is denoted as:

\[ \lim_{{x \to a}} f(x) \]

Here, \( a \) is the value that \( x \) is approaching.


To understand what limits are, let's look at an example. We start with the function \( f(x) = x + 2 \)

The limit of \( f \) at \( x=3 \) is the value \( f \) approaches as we get closer and closer to \( x=3 \). Graphically, this is the \( y \)-value we approach when we look at the graph of \( f \) and get closer and closer to the point on the graph where \( x=3 \).

For example, if we start at the point \( (1,3) \) and move on the graph until we get really close to \( x=3 \), then our \( y \)-value (i.e. the function's value) gets really close to \( 5 \).

Similarly, if we start at \( (5,7) \) and move to the left until we get really close to \( x=3 \), the \( y \)-value again will be really close to \( 5 \).

For these reasons we say that the limit of \( f \) at \( x=3 \) is \( 5 \).

You might be asking yourselves what's the difference between the limit of \( f \) at \( x=3 \) and the value of \( f \) at \( x=3 \), i.e. \( f(3) \).

So yes, the limit of \( f(x)=x+2 \) at \( x=3 \) is equal to \( f(3) \), but this isn't always the case. To understand this, let's look at function \( g \). This function is the same as \( g \) in every way except that it's undefined at \( x=3 \).

Just like \( f \), the limit of \( g \) at \( x=3 \) is \( 5 \). That's because we can still get very very close to \( x=3 \) and the function's values will get very very close to \( 5 \).

So the limit of \( g \) at \( x=3 \) is equal to \( 5 \), but the value of \( g \) at \( x=3 \) is undefined! They are not the same!

That's the beauty of limits: they don't depend on the actual value of the function at the limit. They describe how the function behaves when it gets close to the limit.

Calculating Limits Algebraically

Let's go through the steps of calculating limits algebraically using an example.

Example:

Calculate the limit of the function \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} \) as \( x \) approaches 2.

Step 1: Direct Substitution

We first try to substitute the value into the function. If we can get a defined value, that's our limit.

\[ f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} \]

Substitute \( x = 2 \) into the function:

\[ f(2) = \frac{2^2 - 4}{2 - 2} \]

\[ f(2) = \frac{4 - 4}{0} \]

\[ f(2) = \frac{0}{0} \]

Step 2: Factor and Cancel (if possible)

If we encounter an indeterminate form, we factor the numerator and denominator and see if we can cancel any common factors.

For our function:

\[ f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} \]

Factor the numerator:

\[ f(x) = \frac{(x + 2)(x - 2)}{x - 2} \]

Now we can cancel out \( x - 2 \) from the numerator and denominator:

\[ f(x) = x + 2 \]

Step 3: Direct Substitution (Again)

Now that we have simplified the function, we can try direct substitution again.

\[ f(x) = x + 2 \]

Substitute \( x = 2 \):

\[ f(2) = 2 + 2 \]

\[ f(2) = 4 \]

Answer:

The limit of the function \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} \) as \( x \) approaches 2 is 4.

Explanation:

    - When we first tried to substitute \( x = 2 \), we got an indeterminate form, which means we couldn't find the limit directly.

    - By factoring and canceling, we simplified the function to \( f(x) = x + 2 \), which is defined at \( x = 2 \).

    - So, the limit as \( x \) approaches 2 of \( f(x) \) is simply the value of the function at \( x = 2 \), which is 4.


Another Example:

Let's calculate the limit of \( g(x) = \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} \) as \( x \) approaches 3.

\[ g(x) = \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} \]

Substitute \( x = 3 \):

\[ g(3) = \frac{3^2 - 9}{3 - 3} \]

\[ g(3) = \frac{9 - 9}{0} \]

\[ g(3) = \frac{0}{0} \]

Indeterminate form, so factor and cancel:

\[ g(x) = \frac{(x + 3)(x - 3)}{x - 3} \]

Cancel out \( x - 3 \):

\[ g(x) = x + 3 \]

Substitute \( x = 3 \):

\[ g(3) = 3 + 3 = 6 \]

Conclusion for the Second Example:

The limit of \( g(x) = \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} \) as \( x \) approaches 3 is 6.

One-Sided Limits

A one-sided limit is the value the function approaches as the \( x \)-values approach the limit from one side only. For example, \( f(x)= \frac{|x|}{x} \) returns \( -1 \) for negative numbers, \( 1 \) for positive numbers, and isn't defined for \( 0 \). The one-sided right limit of \( f \) at \( x=0 \) is \( 1 \), and the one-sided left limit at \( x-0 \) is \( -1 \).

This video explores estimating one-sided limit values from graphs. As \( x \) approaches \( 6 \) from the left, the function becomes unbounded with an asymptote, making the left-sided limit nonexistent. However, when approaching \( 6 \) from the right, the function approaches \( -3 \), indicating that the right-handed limit exists.

One-sided limits are used to describe how a function behaves from either the left or right side of a specific point. They are crucial in understanding functions with different behaviors on either side of a point of interest.

Infinite Limits

Basically, a limit must be at a specific point and have a specific value in order to be defined. Nevertheless, there are two kinds of limits that break these rules. One kind is unbounded limits -- limits that approach \( ± \) infinity (you may know them as "vertical asymptotes"). The other kind is limits at infinity -- these limits describe the value a function is approaching as \( x \) goes to \( ± \) infinity (you may know them as "horizontal asymptotes").

An infinite limit occurs when the function's values grow without bound as the \( x \)-values approach a certain value.

This means that as \( x\) gets closer to the specified value, the function values become larger or smaller without limit. We denote this using the symbol \( ∞ \) (infinity) or \( −∞ \) (negative infinity) to represent the infinite behavior.

Consider the function \( g(x) = \frac{1}{x} \):

    As \( x \) approaches 0 from the right (\( x \to 0^+ \)), the function values become larger and larger (positive infinity).

    As \( x \) approaches 0 from the left (\( x \to 0^- \)), the function values become more negative and larger in magnitude (negative infinity).

Infinite Limits for \( g(x) = \frac{1}{x} \) as \( x \) approaches 0:

\( \lim_{{x \to 0^+}} \frac{1}{x} = \infty \)

\( \lim_{{x \to 0^-}} \frac{1}{x} = -\infty \)

Conclusion:

Infinite limits describe the behavior of a function when its values become unbounded (either positive or negative) as the input approaches a certain value.

Limits at Infinity

Limits at infinity describe the behavior of a function as the input \( x \) approaches positive or negative infinity.

This helps us understand what happens to the function's values as \( x \) becomes extremely large (positive infinity) or extremely small (negative infinity).

Example:

Consider the function \( h(x) = \frac{1}{x^2} \):

  • As \( x \) becomes larger and larger (approaching positive infinity), the function values get closer and closer to 0.
  • As \( x \) becomes more negative and large in magnitude (approaching negative infinity), the function values also get closer and closer to 0.

Limits at Infinity for \( h(x) = \frac{1}{x^2} \):

\( \lim_{{x \to \infty}} \frac{1}{x^2} = 0 \) (as \( x \) approaches positive infinity)

\( \lim_{{x \to -\infty}} \frac{1}{x^2} = 0 \) (as \( x \) approaches negative infinity)

Conclusion:

Limits at infinity help us understand the long-term behavior of a function as the input values become extremely large or small.

Limit Laws

Limit laws are rules that allow us to compute limits of more complex functions by breaking them down into simpler parts.

These laws help simplify the process of finding limits algebraically.

Limit Laws:

    1. Sum/Difference Law:

      \( \lim_{{x \to a}} [f(x) + g(x)] = \lim_{{x \to a}} f(x) + \lim_{{x \to a}} g(x) \)

      \( \lim_{{x \to a}} [f(x) - g(x)] = \lim_{{x \to a}} f(x) - \lim_{{x \to a}} g(x) \)

    2. Constant Multiple Law:

      \( \lim_{{x \to a}} [c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot \lim_{{x \to a}} f(x) \)

    3. Product Law:

      \( \lim_{{x \to a}} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = \lim_{{x \to a}} f(x) \cdot \lim_{{x \to a}} g(x) \)

    4. Quotient Law:

      \( \lim_{{x \to a}} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{\lim_{{x \to a}} f(x)}{\lim_{{x \to a}} g(x)} \) (if \( \lim_{{x \to a}} g(x) \neq 0 \))

    5. Power Law:

      \( \lim_{{x \to a}} [f(x)]^n = [\lim_{{x \to a}} f(x)]^n \) (for \( n \) a positive integer)


Conclusion:

Limit laws provide us with a set of rules to simplify the computation of limits for more complex functions by breaking them down into simpler parts. These laws are fundamental in calculus and help us analyze functions' behavior without graphing or direct substitution.
